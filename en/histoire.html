History of Nangeville

Nine centuries of local memory — from the Knights Templar to the Republic, by way of the lords, peasants and soldiers of a small village in the Loiret.

The Coat of Arms of Nangeville

Or, a cross azure cantoned by four lions gules — inspired by the arms of the Hurault family, lords of Nangeville in the 17th century. The lion replaces the Huraults' sun, in reference to the tombstone of Pierre de Nangeville, first sergeant of the king's bodyguard.

A singular religious history

For a village of this size, Nangeville has maintained surprisingly rich links with the religious history of France. As early as the 12th century, a man of Nangeville joined the order of the Knights Templar — those warrior-monks who left their mark on all of medieval Christendom. It is the oldest known trace of a villager involved in the great affairs of the kingdom.

Religious vocations. The parish registers mention, in the 19th century, women born in Nangeville who entered religious life and devoted themselves to their community — a sign of the central place the Church then held in the village's daily life.
Sources: parish registers · Archives départementales du Loiret · oral traditions collected locally.

A village of peasants in the 19th century

In the 19th century, Nangeville was above all a land of tilling and stock-rearing. The agricultural censuses of the time record an impressive herd: between 3,000 and 4,000 head of livestock — sheep, cows, horses and poultry combined — for a human population that, according to the censuses of the time, did not exceed a few hundred souls.

This ratio — nearly thirty animals per inhabitant — says everything about a rural economy centred on the land, where every family lived to the rhythm of harvests, herds and seasons. It is in this peasant world that the 1850 parish survey takes root, recording even the everyday superstitions, such as the ban on doing laundry on Fridays.

The change in population (1831–2022)

Censuses · Cassini-EHESS / INSEE

The history of Nangeville can also be read in its population figures. Like a large share of French villages, it experienced a demographic peak in the 19th century, then a long decline linked to the rural exodus, before a recent stabilisation. The figures below are census counts (not estimates); for so small a population, year-to-year variations often reflect only the movement of a few families.

The Napoleonic cadastre: a village of fields and woods

From 1807, Napoleon ordered a parcel-by-parcel cadastre for the whole of France, completed around 1850. Its purpose was practical: to know precisely each plot of land — its area, its nature, its owner — in order to establish a fair land tax. Now more than two centuries old, this document has become a precious historical source for understanding how a village's land was organised at the time.

How was the commune divided? The cadastre divided the territory of Nangeville into sections designated by a letter (A, B, C…), themselves subdivided into numbered parcels. Each parcel was classified by its nature: arable land (cultivated field), wood or coppice (managed forest), meadow, garden, building… This snapshot of the village in the 19th century shows which areas were cultivated, which were wooded, and how the farms were distributed across the territory.
Consulting the plans. The complete plans of the Napoleonic cadastre of Nangeville can be consulted at the Archives départementales du Loiret. If you hold copies or extracts of these plans, feel free to share them via our Contact page — they would considerably enrich this page.
Note on present-day land ownership. Out of respect for the privacy of current owners, this site does not present information on present-day land ownership. Modern cadastral information remains officially available from the town-planning department of the Le Malesherbois town hall or on the official cadastre site (cadastre.gouv.fr).
Source: Archives départementales du Loiret (Napoleonic cadastral plans, Nangeville section).
