Landscape and habitats

Nangeville lies in the heart of the Gâtinais, a natural region straddling the Loiret and the Essonne, characterised by a mix of large arable plains and forest massifs. This landscape has shaped daily life in the village for centuries.

An open landscape, between fields and woods. Around Nangeville, large cereal crops dominate a gently rolling plateau. These open plains are dotted with hedges, copses and small woods, remnants of the traditional Gâtinais hedgerow landscape. It is these habitats — cultivated field, woodland edge, hedge, meadow, water point — that make up the web of wildlife across the area.
The role of hedges. In a landscape of large crops, hedges and grass strips play a major ecological role: they serve as shelter, movement corridor and larder for many species. This is a general characteristic of hedgerow landscapes, well documented at the regional and national level.
These descriptions reflect the general characteristics of the Gâtinais / Centre-Val de Loire. They do not constitute a survey carried out in Nangeville itself.

Flora

The vegetation associated with this type of arable and hedgerow landscape is that, typical of the region, of large crops, hedges and roadsides. The species mentioned below are characteristic of the Gâtinais and of the northern half of France; they are not the result of a botanical survey specific to the village.

The crops. The plateau is dominated by the large cereal and oilseed crops characteristic of the region: wheat, barley, rapeseed, and sometimes maize, sugar beet or sunflower depending on crop rotation. These are what trace, season after season, the dominant colour of the landscape.
Hedges and woodland edges. The region's hedgerows typically bring together species such as hawthorn, blackthorn, hazel, elder, field maple and dogwood. These shrubs offer flowers in spring and berries in autumn, a precious resource for birds and insects.
Field and wayside flowers. Along crop margins and banks, one commonly finds, in this type of habitat, typical arable and ruderal plants: poppy, cornflower, oxeye daisy, wild chicory, yarrow, as well as various wild grasses. Several of these arable-weed species (companions of the harvest) are becoming scarcer at the national level.
Species cited as representative examples of the region's arable and hedgerow landscapes. A precise local inventory would require field surveys; naturalist contributions are welcome via the Contact page.

Fauna and insects

The fauna associated with this type of landscape — large crops, hedges, woods and water points — is likewise typical of the region. The species below are characteristic of the arable habitats of the northern Loiret; this is not a survey carried out in Nangeville.

Mammals and birds. The landscape is home to the common fauna of cultivated plains: roe deer, hares, foxes and small mammals. Above the stubble glide the raptors typical of the region — common buzzards, kites, kestrels — while the hedges host many songbirds. The skylark, the partridge and the bunting are among the emblematic birds of these open habitats.
Insects and pollinators. The region's arable and hedgerow habitats host a great diversity of insects: honeybees and wild bees, bumblebees, butterflies (swallowtail, cabbage white, peacock, painted lady), ladybirds, grasshoppers and crickets, as well as dragonflies near water points. These insects play an essential pollination role and form the first link in the food chain for many birds.

Trends and change

National data

No naturalist monitoring has been carried out specifically in Nangeville. At the national level, however, there are long-term scientific monitoring programmes that document changes in the biodiversity of the countryside — and which directly concern the arable habitats that Nangeville is part of.

A national trend — not a measurement made in Nangeville. In France, birds that specialise in arable habitats have seen their populations fall by about one third in thirty years. The national reference index (STOC monitoring) records a decline of −36% between 1989 and 2021.

Why does this concern Nangeville? These figures are national, but they describe precisely the type of habitat that surrounds the village: the large cereal-growing plains of the Gâtinais. Trends measured at the country level therefore also concern, very directly, the arable landscapes characteristic of our region. The causes identified by scientists — agricultural intensification, the scarcity of insects and arable plants, the disappearance of hedges — are those at work across the French countryside as a whole.
