Memory of the families

The history of Nangeville is also that of the families who lived, worked and shaped the village over the generations — told from local archives and handed-down memories.

The Huet family

Among the families whose name has remained attached to Nangeville, the Huets hold a leading place. Their story, documented by archives and the local press, runs through the whole of the 19th century — from the purchase of the farm to agricultural distinctions, and on to the village town hall.

After the Revolution, the Nangeville farm was sold as a national asset for 24,000 francs to three buyers, among them Valentin Huet. The family thus put down lasting roots in the village's land.

On 10 June 1865, Mr Huet, of Nangeville, was awarded a silver medal for asparagus growing and a gold medal for the good upkeep of his farm — proof of the household's agricultural know-how.

On 20 May 1871, Mr Huet was appointed mayor of Nangeville, assisted by his deputy, Mr Branche.

The "Memoirs of Théodore Huet" are preserved as a documentary source in the village archives, a precious testimony to the life of a Nangeville family.

Source: local press archives, via Nangeville Village.

The d'Adonville family

The d'Adonvilles are among the seigneurial families whose presence in Nangeville is best documented for the modern period. Their history in the village, closely tied to that of the great families of the Gâtinais, spans the 16th to the 18th century.

Entry into the seigneury through marriage (around 1575–1579). It was through a marriage that the d'Adonvilles settled in Nangeville: Jacques d'Adonville married Philippe (Bardin), a woman from the d'Outarville family — a male first name then borne by women in certain families of the region. Through this marriage, Jacques d'Adonville became co-lord (seigneur en partie) of the Tour Carrée, one of the fiefs of Nangeville, thus entering the circle of the village's co-lords.
A short-lived landholding, a lasting small fief. The family seems to have sold off most of its Nangeville holdings fairly quickly — probably to the Hurault de l'Hospital family (of Vignay) around 1584. It kept, however, a small fief, that of la Galoise, which it held until around 1680. This partial retention shows a real attachment to the area, even after the sale of the main lands.
A late seigneurial title (1717). By the end of the 17th century, no fief seems directly associated with the d'Adonvilles in Nangeville. Yet in 1717, Estienne d'Adonville is still described as co-lord (seigneur en partie) of Nangeville in the records. This title, maintained without any obvious landed basis, reflects the survival of symbolic or residual seigneurial rights, common in Ancien Régime France.
Archives and genealogy. A genealogical tree of the d'Adonville family covering the 16th–19th centuries, including the Nangeville branch, was compiled by the local researcher Roger Huet. Various archive items complete this picture: marriage contracts (1653), property sales (1586) and other documents that make it possible to reconstruct the family's trajectory. These sources are referenced on the site nangevillevillage.fr.
Source: Nangeville Village (nangevillevillage.fr), d'Adonville genealogy by Roger Huet.

Other documented families

In its Families and transmission section, the Nangeville Village association lists several other families whose traces are preserved in the local archives. The notes below are cautious summaries, based on the referenced section titles — for full detail, consult nangevillevillage.fr.

The de Nangeville family
Among the oldest names attached to the village, the de Nangeville family most likely adopted the name of the place as its surname — a common practice in the medieval nobility. A seal in the name of Thibaut de Nangeville appears among the medieval documents referenced by the local archives, a testimony to this ancient presence in the great affairs of the territory.
The Hurault de l'Hôpital family
The Hurault de l'Hôpital were the lords of Nangeville in the 17th century, and it was their arms that inspired the village coat of arms adopted in 1995. A section devoted to their presence in the early 18th century is referenced in the Nangeville Village archives — a pivotal period between the Ancien Régime and the upheavals to come.
The Cottereau family
The Nangeville Village archives mention a "Cottereau Collection" — a set of documents or archive items linked to this family and kept or referenced by the association. The precise content of this collection is not detailed here; to find out more, consult nangevillevillage.fr directly.
The Arnoul family
As with the Cottereau family, an "Arnoul Collection" is referenced by the Nangeville Village association in its section devoted to families and transmission. These collections reflect the work of gathering and preservation carried out locally to safeguard the memory of Nangeville's families.
The Mesmin family
The Mesmin family is among those documented in the Families and transmission section of Nangeville Village. A section is devoted to it in the local archives, the precise content of which is not reproduced here. To consult the available documents, refer to nangevillevillage.fr.
The Chauvet family
A lawyer's brief (mémoire d'avocat) from 1787 — two years before the Revolution — is the key surviving document concerning the Chauvet family. This legal piece, drawn up on the eve of a world about to be overturned, is a striking window onto the life and affairs of a Nangeville family at the end of the Ancien Régime.
The last bearers of the Nangeville name.

In the Middle Ages, it was common for the nobility to bear the name of the place from which they held their seigneury — and so families "de Nangeville" came into being. Over the centuries, through marriages, the extinction of male lines and changes of regime, these family names linked to the village gradually disappeared. The Nangeville Village archives document who were the last to bear the surname "de Nangeville" — a question that touches on genealogy, local history and the way a place name does, or does not, survive in human memory. The village's name, for its part, endures.

Source: Nangeville Village (nangevillevillage.fr), Families and transmission section.

Other families, other stories

The Huets are just one family among those that made Nangeville. From generation to generation, many families have forged ties with the village — through the land, work, alliances or simply the memory of a place of origin. The Lefebvre family, for example, is among those whose local roots are still passed down today.

This page is meant to grow. Every family carries a part of the village's history: a trade, a farm, a name carved on a monument, a faded photograph. As families share their memories, these accounts will enrich, page after page, the collective memory of Nangeville.

Genealogical trees

Detailed genealogical trees for several historic Nangeville families — notably the d'Adonvilles, whose Nangeville branch is documented from the 16th to the 19th century — have been compiled by local researchers, in particular Roger Huet, and are referenced on the site nangevillevillage.fr. This work of collection represents a precious genealogical heritage for anyone wishing to trace their Nangeville roots.

This site prefers to point to the original sources rather than reproduce these trees in full, for two reasons: first, fidelity to the original research, which deserves to be fully credited; and second, the protection of privacy of recent generations — personal data relating to living people or people born in the 20th century is protected by the GDPR and could not appear here without the explicit consent of those concerned.

This site therefore focuses on historical figures (before the 20th century) for whom archive documents are available and freely shareable. If you are yourself descended from a Nangeville family and would like to share your own family tree — with your explicit consent — write to us: we will be happy to include it in a future update, with full credit to you.
